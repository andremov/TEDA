\begin{abstract}
The Ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF) and cosmological parameter inference share a common statistical bottleneck: both require the inverse of a covariance matrix estimated from a small number of realizations. We embed the direct precision shrinkage framework of Bodnar et al.\ inside the dual formulation of the EnKF, recomputing the shrinkage coefficients at every assimilation cycle, and benchmark six estimators (four direct precision shrinkage targets, Ledoit--Wolf covariance shrinkage, and NERCOME) on two systems with opposite precision structure: Lorenz~96 with dense off-diagonal precision and BOSS DR12 power-spectrum mocks with near-diagonal precision. Two findings emerge. First, target-truth alignment is decisive: the direct precision methods achieve the lowest Frobenius loss on cosmological data but fail catastrophically on Lorenz~96, where unclamped $\beta^*$ values reach $10^8$--$10^9$ and clamping occurs in over 80\% of cycles. Second, standalone estimation accuracy does not imply good filtering: Ledoit--Wolf gives the worst standalone precision estimates on cosmological data yet achieves the lowest EnKF RMSE, because its low cycle-to-cycle variance compounds favourably over hundreds of assimilation steps. Variance reduction, not bias reduction, is the dominant criterion for sequential filtering. We also propose a domain-agnostic scaled-identity target that sits between the plain identity and the domain-specific cosmological target.

\end{abstract}
